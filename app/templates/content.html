Introduction

Computer assembly can seem quite overwhelming at first look due both the apparent complexity and the fragile components but this short course will help you understand how simple and easy it really is to build your own computer ignoring tools you will need all of the basic components of a computer. A CPU, Compatible Motherboard, GPU, RAM, storage, coolers, Power Suppy, a Case and any other parts you want to have.

This course will go through the assembly process of a computer part by part so that each parts purpose and correct method of installation can be understood.

You will need

  • Philips Head screwdrivers
  • Zip ties for calbe managment
  • Flashlight for dark parts of the case
  • Thermal paste if not using stock coolers
  • A place to store your screws

Some people choose to use anti-static equipment to ensure their hardware is protected, while this is optional it is advisable to take precautions in relation to static as you could unintentionally damage your hardware a simple way to do this is to have components rest on their static bags while being prepared for installation

Attaching Components to the Motherboard

While some people prefer to attach the motherboard before other components it is commonly easier to fit components when it is yet to be inserted into the case so there is more space.

The CPU

for installing most CPU's no matter the brand the first step is to release the tension lever to gain access to the CPU socket (The difference between Intel and AMD CPU's in this regard is Intel has pins in the socket and AMD has pins on the cpu)

The alignment indicator (arrow or triangle) lines up with the one on the socket or socket cover. Failing to do so could result in damage to the CPU, Motherboard or Both. Simply relase the CPU into the slot and it should settle in on its own if it fails to do so, simply reseat it DO NOT try to force the CPU into the slot as you certainly going to damage something.

This guide covers standard types of cores but it is important to note that parts like Intel Core X series and AMD Threadripper series CPU's have special installation processes and how those CPU's are installed is not covered in this guide.

CPU Cooler

Most processors come with their own cooler out of the box, unless you are seriously overclocking or want a special style this cooler is normally acceptable for use

If you choose to use a stock cooler, you will find the thermal paste already applied. With an aftermarket cooler this is generally not the case and you will need to apply your own thermal paste. When applying thermal paste it is important to understand that you only need a very small amount of paste, about a pea sized drop in the centre of the CPU. There are special techniques that serious assemblers use to spread the paste as evenly as possible, but in our case it is fine to simply let the cooler spread the paste when attached. Make sure to not add too much paste as you don't want it squirting out and onto the socket and PCB.

Coolers use different types of installation depending on the manufacturer and type, most stock coolers use either a push pin system which is commonly seen on intel coolers or bar based lock in system as seen on AMD coolers. For Push pin coolers it is important to push on the opposite corners to spread the thermal paste as evenly as possible. Aftermarket coolers are installed in many different ways so it is important to consult the manufacturers instructions as the installation method can be quite complex.

After installing the CPU cooler remember to attach the CPU cooler cable to the cpu cooler power connector on the motherboard commonly labeled something like "CPU_FAN"

The Memory

Installing RAM is a simple task. First you must open the latches on the RAM slots, most boards have two latches but some more budget boards have just one. Once the latches are open, simply align the DIMM chip with the slot, ensuring the divot in the chip aligns with the bump on the board then simply push the RAM chip in, this may require a bit of force but if it takes a notable amount of trouble ensure that the chip is aligned properly with the slot. When put in correctly the latches will snap into place and lock the chip in place. When installing RAM chips remember to install into the correct slots so that the motherboard recognises them, this is done by installing the chip pairs into slots with the same data channel. WIth motherboards that use 4 slots this is commonly split into the odd and even slots.

M.2 SSD

If you have a M.2 SSD now is a good time to install it as parts may get in its way later.

If it is already installed remove the screw located across form the m.2 slot and slide the SSD in at an angle. make sure the notch lines up, similar to how a RAM chip is installed. If it does not line up there may be a compatability issue between the drive and the slot. Slowly lay the SSD down and install the mounting screw.

Installing the Motherboard to the Case

Now that we have built the core of the computer (minus the graphics card), it is time to install the motherboard into the case. If it is not yet done, remove the side panels of the case to gain access to the installation area.

Standoffs

First, gather the standoff screws that came with your case. Then determine the correct location for the standoff screws in your case. This is determined my the size of your motherboard and where its screw holes are. It is important that there are only standofs where needed and nowhere else as standoffs in incorrect locations could damage the motherboard.

I/O Shield

The I/O shield which covers the area around your motherboards rear ports, comes with the motherboard. You need to fit the I/O shield before mounting the motherboard itself making sure it is in the correct orientation so the motherboard ports will fit through the holes in the shield. You will have to use some force to snap the I/O shield into place. Be carefull of the sharp edges as they can cause injuries during installation. If you have a board with a pre-installed IO shield you do not need to do this step.

The Motherboard

It is finally time to install the motherboard itself. Make sure that the holes on the motherboard line up with the standoffs and that the ports line up with the I/O shield. Once in place put the screws into the standoffs to hold the motherboard in place.

Power Supply and Traditional Storage

Now for the parts that aren't on the motherboard itself

Power Supply

The PSU is normally mounted at the back of the case, most commonly it sits at the bottom of the case but can sometimes be found at the top. The installation process is simple as the power supply simply needs to be attached to the back of the case. After that the first power connectors are attached. Those being the 24 pin power connector and the supplemental power connector, both attach to the motherboard.

SATA Storage

Storage drives are installed by attaching the SATA cable from the motherboard SATA slot to the 2.5 or 3.5 drive as well as attaching their SATA power connector from the PSU. They are installed into the case through unique mounting methods per case so please refer to the case instructions for specifics

Installing the Graphics Card

This step is only needed if either your CPU lacks integrated graphics or you plan or doing more graphically intensive tasks like PC gaming. To install a graphics card first the slot covers on the back must be removed to give space for the GPU's rear slots. After this is done the GPU is placed into the PCIe x16 slot on the motherboard this is the long and thin slot normally closest under the CPU. If needed you may have to also attached PCIe power connectors form the PSU to connectors on the graphics card. Installing any other PCIe based card like a wifi card is done using a similar process (look for the slots that match the size of the cards connector).

The Final Cables

This is the last major step the cables that come off the case must be attached to the motherboard. These connect to points like the audio connectors, USB2.0 and 3.0 ports and the motherboards front panel I/O connectors. It is important to ensure they are going in the right place as these cables are small and they have very small connectors to mount to. Refer to your motherboards manual for the exact location of the ports you are connecting cables to.

In Conclusion

With this your system is almost done. if you have any extra cooling install them by the instruction in the manual and perform cleanup on your cables. You can then attach periferals and attempt start up. To truly finish you need to install your OS and drivers this is done differently depending on your desired OS but remember to follow the instructions the OS provider gives for installation.
